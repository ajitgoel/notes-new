Based on the video’s framework — and expanding on it — here are practical ways to train your subconscious mind:

**1. Use Repetition Deliberately** 

- **Build identity-level habits.** Pick one behavior you want to become automatic and do it daily, no exceptions, for at least 60–90 days. The subconscious doesn’t care about your intentions — it responds to what you actually do repeatedly.
- **Consume intentionally.** What you read, watch, and listen to on repeat feeds your subconscious. If you scroll negativity for 2 hours a day, that’s what gets embedded. Replace it with material aligned with what you want to become.
- **Use affirmations — but with a twist.** Saying “I am confident” while feeling insecure doesn’t work because the subconscious detects the mismatch. Instead, use evidence-based statements: “I handled that difficult conversation well last week.” Real memories stick.
- **Visualize before sleep.** Right before sleep, your brain transitions from conscious to subconscious processing. Spend 5 minutes vividly imagining yourself performing well at something you’re working toward — your subconscious processes it overnight.

**2. Seek Uniqueness and Novelty** 

- **Break routines on purpose.** Take a different route, eat something unfamiliar, talk to someone outside your usual circle. Small novelty keeps the subconscious alert and receptive.
- **Travel — even locally.** You don’t need a plane ticket. Visit a neighborhood you’ve never been to, attend an event outside your field, sit in on a lecture about something you know nothing about.
- **Learn skills from unrelated domains.** A programmer learning pottery, an accountant learning improv comedy — cross-domain learning creates new neural pathways and forces the subconscious to engage.

**3. Put Yourself in Uncomfortable Environments** 

- **Compete above your level.** Join a group where you’re the least experienced person. The discomfort of being “behind” is exactly what trains the subconscious to level up — as the Deepinder Goyal example showed.
- **Do things that make you slightly afraid.** Public speaking, cold outreach, showing your work publicly. The key word is _slightly_ — you want productive discomfort, not paralysis.
- **Set deadlines that force output.** Committing to deliver something by a date — a project, a presentation, a piece of writing — creates the pressure that pushes tasks from conscious struggle into subconscious competence.

**4. Protect What’s Already in There** 

This is the part the video hinted at but didn’t fully explore:

- **Be careful with what you repeat casually.** Complaining, self-deprecation, doomscrolling — these are repetition too. Your subconscious doesn’t distinguish between “serious” input and “casual” input. It just registers frequency.
- **Audit your environment.** The people you spend the most time with are a form of repetition. If they reinforce helplessness, your subconscious absorbs that. If they model agency and effort, that gets absorbed instead.
- **Don’t try to fight the subconscious with willpower.** As the video said — once something is embedded, conscious effort alone won’t remove it. To replace a bad subconscious pattern, you need to install a competing pattern using the same three tools: repetition, novelty, and discomfort.

The shortest version: **your subconscious is shaped by what you do repeatedly, what surprises you, and what challenges you. Engineer your daily life around all three, and the subconscious will do the heavy lifting over time.**
