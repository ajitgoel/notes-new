| **Facade Modulation** | Change of materials or min. 18" deep setback at min. every 50 ft |

### Mandatory Housing Affordability (MHA) Requirements

The (M) suffix indicates MHA obligations. Aurora-Licton Springs is classified as a **Medium MHA Payment/Performance Area**. Developers must choose one option:

**Option 1 – Performance (On-Site Affordable Units):**
- Provide **6% of units at 60% AMI** for residential component
- For ~130 units = ~**8 affordable units** required on-site
- Allows faster permitting (skip design review if using performance option)

**Option 2 – Payment (Fee-in-Lieu):**
- Pay ~**$13.25/SF** of residential gross floor area (2025 rate, CPI-adjusted annually)
- For ~87,600 SF residential = ~**$1,160,000 payment**
- This is the more common choice for market-rate projects of this scale

**Recommendation:**

Given the value-oriented positioning and desire for operational simplicity, the **fee-in-lieu (payment option) is recommended**. This allows for optimized market-rate unit mix without the administrative complexity of income-restricted units and provides more straightforward underwriting for conventional financing.

This Aurora location appeals to specific renter segments prioritizing **value and transit access** over premium finishes:

Healthcare workers (UW Medicine, Swedish Northgate), retail/food service employees, tradespeople. **Income: $40-60K**. Prefer studios and 1BRs priced $1,100-1,500. Value transit access to multiple job sites.

Single parents, young families. Need 2BR or 3BR units. **Driven by affordability** — willing to trade neighborhood prestige for extra space. Proximity to schools (Robert Eagle Staff, North Seattle elementary options) is moderate positive.

## 4. OPTIMAL UNIT MIX & SIZING STRATEGY

Unit mix is driven by three competing objectives: (1) maximize rent/SF from smaller units, (2) provide flexibility for diverse household sizes, and (3) achieve efficient floor plate layouts. Based on comparable performance and demographic analysis, we recommend a **balanced mix weighted toward studios and 1BRs** with moderate 2BR and limited 3BR representation.

### Recommended Unit Mix (130 Total Units)

| **Unit Type** | **Count** | **% of Total** | **Avg Size (SF)** | **Target Rent** | **Rent/SF** |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Studio | 26 | 20% | 450 | $1,100 | $2.44 |
| 1BR | 65 | 50% | 620 | $1,450 | $2.34 |
| 2BR / 1BA | 32 | 25% | 950 | $2,250 | $2.37 |
| 3BR / 2BA | 7 | 5% | 1,200 | $2,800 | $2.33 |
| **TOTAL / WEIGHTED AVG** | **130** | **100%** | **~580 SF** | **$1,370** | **$2.35** |

### Unit Mix Rationale

#### Studios (26 units, 20%)
Strong demand from single workers, students. **Highest rent/SF**. Size range: 420-480 SF. Mix of interior (cheaper) and corner/view (premium). Sufficient to capture segment without oversaturation.

#### 1-Bedrooms (65 units, 50%)
**Core product**. Serves singles, couples, roommates. Size range: 580-680 SF with some 1BR+den layouts (~720 SF) commanding $1,550-1,600. This is the **most liquid unit type** with broadest demand. Balance of standard and premium layouts.

#### 2-Bedrooms (32 units, 25%)
Couples, small families, roommate pairs. Size range: 900-1,000 SF, all 1 bath (2 baths adds cost without material rent premium in this market). Sufficient to avoid turning away family demand, but limited to prevent vacancy risk.

#### 3-Bedrooms (7 units, 5%)
Families, roommate groups (e.g., UW students splitting rent). Size: 1,150-1,250 SF, 2 baths. **Limited count** reflects narrow demand pool but prevents leaving meaningful revenue on table. Slower turnover but stable when leased.

### Alternative Mix Scenarios Considered

#### Scenario A: Micro-Unit Strategy (Higher Density)
- **Concept**: Increase studios to 35-40% with smaller units (380-420 SF), add SEDUs (small efficiency dwelling units)
- **Total Units**: ~150-160
- **Pros**: Higher rent/SF ($2.60-2.80), maximizes revenue per building SF, appeals to ultra-budget renters
- **Cons**: Seattle SEDU regulations restrictive, narrow market appeal, higher turnover, quality concerns for location

**Recommendation: REJECT.** *Aurora location already has perception challenges; micro-units exacerbate "cheap" positioning and limit upgrade path.*

#### Scenario B: Family-Oriented Mix (More 2BR/3BR)
- **Concept**: Reduce studios to 10%, increase 2BR to 35% and 3BR to 15%
- **Total Units**: ~100-110
- **Pros**: Longer lease terms, lower turnover, premium per unit
- **Cons**: Lower rent/SF, families prefer Green Lake/Wallingford schools over Aurora, vacancy risk on large units

**Recommendation: REJECT.** *Demographics don't support this; workforce/student tenants dominate demand in this corridor.*

## 8. DESIGN & POSITIONING STRATEGY (AURORA-SPECIFIC)

### Challenges of Aurora Avenue Location

1. **Traffic & Noise**: 6-lane arterial with 35 MPH speed limit, heavy truck traffic
2. **Safety Perception**: Higher crime rates than Green Lake/Wallingford; visible homelessness, auto-oriented
3. **Aesthetic**: Strip malls, used car lots, motels; lacks walkable charm
4. **Air Quality**: Highway proximity raises concerns (though improving with EV adoption)

### Mitigation Strategies Through Design

#### Building Orientation & Unit Layout

- **Orient Premium Units to Nesbit Ave N** (quieter side street)
  - Corner units, larger 1BR+den, 2BR/3BR units face away from Aurora
  - Aurora-facing units are primarily studios and small 1BRs (lower price point, less noise sensitivity)

- **Courtyard Configuration** (if site allows):
  - U-shaped or L-shaped building with interior courtyard
  - Provides quiet outdoor space shielded from Aurora traffic
  - Balconies face courtyard, not Aurora

#### Sound Attenuation

- **Enhanced Window Systems** for Aurora-facing units:
  - Dual-pane, laminated glass (STC 38-40 minimum)
  - Reduces traffic noise by 10-15 decibels vs. standard windows
  - Cost: +$2,000-3,000/unit for Aurora facade

- **Wall Assemblies**:
  - Continuous insulation (ci) on Aurora facade exceeds code minimum
  - Reduces both noise and thermal transfer

#### Security & CPTED (Crime Prevention Through Environmental Design)

- **Controlled Access Points**: Single main entry on Nesbit (off Aurora); secondary emergency exits only
- **Lobby Visibility**: Large windows, well-lit, visible from street (natural surveillance)
- **Landscape Design**: Avoid tall hedges/hiding spots; use ornamental grasses, low shrubs
- **Lighting**: Bright, motion-activated lights at all entries, parking areas, pathways
- **Security Cameras**: Cover all entries, parking, bike storage (posted signage as deterrent)

#### Facade & Street Presence

- **Facade Modulation**: Break up mass with material changes, balcony setbacks every 50 ft (per code)
- **Ground-Floor Activation**: Commercial/live-work fronting Aurora creates "eyes on the street"
- **Residential Entry on Nesbit**: Separates residential from highway; quieter, more inviting
- **Landscaping Buffer**: 10-15 ft setback with trees, bioswales along Aurora (per NC3P requirements)

### Marketing & Positioning Narrative

Given Aurora's challenges, **messaging is critical** to attract quality tenants. Avoid defensive tone; emphasize positives.

#### Positioning Statement (Example)

> **"Transit-Connected Living in North Seattle"**
>
> Experience the perfect blend of affordability and accessibility at [Project Name]. Located on the RapidRide E Line with direct access to Northgate Link, UDistrict, and Downtown, our modern apartments offer the space and value you need without the premium price tag of adjacent neighborhoods. Enjoy a vibrant, evolving corridor with easy access to Green Lake trails, Northgate restaurants, and Seattle's best employers—all while living in a thoughtfully designed building with the amenities that matter.

#### Key Messaging Points

- **"Minutes from Everywhere"**: RapidRide E Line (5-min walk), Northgate Station (5-min bus)
- **"More Space, Less Rent"**: 10-15% below Green Lake/Wallingford for equivalent quality
- **"Evolving Neighborhood"**: Aurora transformation underway (new developments, transit investments)
- **"Outdoor Access"**: Green Lake Park (1 mile), Burke-Gilman Trail (1.5 miles), Woodland Park (2 miles)
- **"Employer Proximity"**: UW Medicine, Swedish Northgate, North Seattle College, downtown commute

#### Avoid Mentioning

- Aurora's reputation issues (don't apologize or acknowledge negatives)
- Distance from "premium" neighborhoods (focus on proximity to amenities, not distance from others)
- Defensive language ("We're not like other Aurora buildings...")

## 9. PHASING, TIMING & DELIVERY STRATEGY

### Recommended Development Timeline

| **Phase** | **Duration** | **Completion Date** | **Key Activities** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Pre-Development** | 6-9 months | Q3 2026 | Entitlements, final design, financing, GC selection |
| **Permitting** | 12-18 months | Q1 2028 | SEPA, MHA election, building permit (assume payment option = no design review) |
| **Construction** | 18-22 months | Q3-Q4 2029 | Site prep, podium, wood-frame, interiors, finishes |
| **Lease-Up** | 6-9 months | Q2 2030 | Marketing, pre-leasing (start 90 days before delivery), stabilization |
| **Stabilization** | 12 months | Q2 2031 | 95%+ occupancy, refinance or hold |

**Target Delivery: Q3-Q4 2029**

### Market Timing Considerations

#### Why 2029 Delivery is Optimal

1. **Current Pipeline Absorption** (2026-2028):
   - Northgate Station projects (M2/M3, Victory) deliver 2026-27
   - Allows 12-18 months for those projects to stabilize before subject competes
   - By 2029, they'll be seasoned product at higher rents; subject enters as "newest option"

2. **Economic Cycle Positioning**:
   - Current construction slowdown (2025-2026) means limited new supply 2027-2028
   - Subject benefits from supply gap if construction doesn't accelerate
   - Rent growth likely to continue at modest 2-3% annually through 2029

3. **Transit Maturation**:
   - Northgate Link opened Oct 2021; by 2029, it's 8 years mature (ridership stabilized)
   - RapidRide E Line improvements likely implemented (current SDOT plans)
   - Transit-oriented development (TOD) premium will be better understood, priced

4. **Financing Environment**:
   - Current high interest rates (2025-2026) expected to moderate by 2027-2028
   - Better debt pricing for construction/perm loans if starting in 2026-2027

#### Risks to Timing

- **Permitting Delays**: Seattle SDCI backlogged; 18-month estimate could stretch to 24 months
  - Mitigation: Elect MHA payment option (skip design review), engage expediter, pre-submittal meetings
  
- **Construction Cost Escalation**: If labor/materials spike again (2028-2029), pro forma at risk
  - Mitigation: Lock GC contract with cost caps, escalation clauses, alternative materials

- **Competing Aurora Projects**: If other developers see same opportunity, Aurora corridor could oversupply
  - Mitigation: Monitor permits quarterly; if competitors emerge, consider accelerating timeline

### Phasing Recommendation

**Single-Phase Development (Recommended)**

- Entire 130 units delivered at once (Q3-Q4 2029)
- **Pros**: Economies of scale, single financing package, faster stabilization
- **Cons**: Higher capital requirement, all-or-nothing risk

**Multi-Phase Not Recommended** for this site size; only 0.46 acres isn't large enough to justify phasing. Phasing typically requires 2+ acres to spread risk and infrastructure costs.

## 10. CLEAR FINAL RECOMMENDATION

### If This Were My Project: The Building I Would Design

**PROJECT CONCEPT: "Aurora Station Apartments"**

I would develop a **6-story, wood-frame podium building with 130 units** positioned as **"transit-connected value"** for workforce renters who prioritize affordability and mobility over neighborhood prestige. Here's the specific program:

#### The Numbers

- **Total Units**: 130 (26 studios, 65 1BRs, 32 2BRs, 7 3BRs)
- **Average Unit Size**: 580 SF
- **Target Rent**: $1,370/unit average, $2.35/SF
- **Parking**: 65 spaces (0.5 ratio), unbundled at $75/month
- **Ground Floor**: 3,500 SF shallow commercial (coffee shop ideal), 1,200 SF residential lobby
- **Projected EGI Year 2**: $2.48M ($19,052/unit/year)

#### The Design

**Building Form:**
- L-shaped or U-shaped configuration with units wrapping a landscaped courtyard
- Residential entry on Nesbit Ave N (quieter side); commercial fronts Aurora
- Ground floor: Tuck-under parking (35 spaces) + commercial + lobby
- Floors 2-6: Typical residential floors (~26 units/floor)

**Unit Distribution by Floor:**
- Ground floor: 0 residential units (all parking/commercial/amenities)
- Floors 2-6: ~26 units/floor × 5 floors = 130 units
  - Each floor: 5-6 studios, 13-14 1BRs, 6-7 2BRs, 1-2 3BRs (flex by floor)

**Facade Strategy:**
- **Aurora Side**: Continuous balconies (sound buffer), laminated glass, material modulation every 50 ft
- **Nesbit/Courtyard Side**: Larger windows, Juliet balconies, warmer materials (fiber cement, wood accents)
- **Color Palette**: Grays, blues, whites (modern, clean); avoid dated brick or stucco

**Amenities (Cost-Effective Package):**
- 1,200 SF fitness center (ground floor, windows to courtyard)
- 300 SF coworking nook (2nd floor, overlooks courtyard)
- 1,800 SF courtyard with seating, BBQs, dog relief area
- Package room (300 SF), bike storage (50 bikes), pet wash station

**In-Unit Features:**
- In-unit W/D (all units)
- Ductless mini-split HVAC (heat pumps)
- Luxury vinyl plank flooring, laminate counters, thermofoil cabinets
- Open layouts, 9-ft ceilings, large windows

#### The Underwriting (High-Level)

**Development Costs (Order of Magnitude):**
- Hard Costs: $25-30M ($190-230/SF all-in)
  - Site work, podium, wood-frame, finishes at value-grade specs
- Soft Costs: $4-5M (architecture, engineering, permits, fees, MHA payment $1.16M, financing, marketing)
- **Total Development Cost**: ~$29-35M (~$223-269K/unit)

**Stabilized Returns (Year 2):**
- Effective Gross Income: $2.48M
- Operating Expenses: $975K (39% of EGI; property mgmt, utilities, maintenance, taxes, insurance)
- **Net Operating Income (NOI)**: $1.50M
- **Cap Rate (assumed exit)**: 5.5-6.0% (North Seattle mid-tier product)
- **Stabilized Value**: $25-27M (at 6.0% cap: $1.50M / 0.06 = $25M)

**This is a value-add play, not a home run.** The returns are modest (mid-teens IRR likely), but the fundamentals are solid: strong demographic demand, manageable competition, favorable zoning, and a clear positioning gap in the market.

#### Why This Works

1. **Underserved Niche**: Few new market-rate apartments on Aurora despite strong zoning
2. **Demographic Fit**: Workforce/student renters need this price point ($1,100-1,450/month)
3. **Transit Tailwind**: RapidRide + Northgate Link make car-free living viable (reduces parking cost)
4. **MHA Payment Option**: Simplifies operations vs. on-site affordable units
5. **Scalable Design**: 130 units achieves efficiency without becoming too large for 0.46-acre site

#### The Risks I'd Watch

- **Aurora Stigma**: Perception is real; budget extra $50-75K for marketing, design to overcome
- **Construction Costs**: Wood-frame prices volatile; lock GC early or use cost-plus with cap
- **Lease-Up Pace**: Plan for 8-10 months to stabilization (vs. 6 months in Green Lake)
- **Competing Projects**: Monitor Northgate pipeline; if M1 (Simon) accelerates, adjust rents down

#### The Bottom Line

This is a **solid, boring, cash-flowing multifamily project** in a market with strong fundamentals and weak competition. It's not sexy—you're not building on the waterfront or next to a tech campus—but that's exactly why it works. The renters you're targeting don't need sexy; they need clean, safe, affordable, and transit-connected. Deliver that at $2.35/SF, and you'll lease up.

**I would build this.**
