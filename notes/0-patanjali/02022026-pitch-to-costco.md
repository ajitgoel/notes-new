**Step 2: Make Your Offer “Costco-Ready”** 

Before you pitch, tighten your offer so it sounds like something Costco can drop on a pallet tomorrow.

Focus your Costco offer on **one hero SKU**:  
**Patanjali-style 5L Cow Ghee, sold as a 2-pack**.

Have answers ready for:

- **Item details:** exact net weight, pack size (2 × 5L), case pack, pallet configuration (cases per layer, layers per pallet, total units per pallet).
- **Wholesale price:** your delivered price per case to their regional DC or Business Center.
- **Suggested retail:** what you think they can retail it for while hitting their margin.
- **Margin:** show that your price leaves Costco a healthy gross margin, ideally 20–30%+ at Business Center.
- **Certifications:** FSSAI, any US-side lab tests, vegetarian logo, etc.
- **Compliance:** clarity on labeling (English, nutrition facts, ingredients, allergen declaration, country of origin).
- **Supply:** how many cases per month you can reliably supply and from which US warehouse (Everett, WA).

Costco buyers care about: **margin, velocity, reliability, and risk.** Your pitch has to hit all four.

Costco won’t commit off a “we can probably get it in 3 months” story, but they are used to 3–6 month lead times if you present it as a **planned, de‑risked launch window** rather than a vague future plan.

**How they’re likely to think about it** 

From a buyer’s perspective, they’ll ask:

- Is the product real and compliant?
- Is the supply chain locked in (factory, shipping, US warehouse)?
- Is the US entity ready to ship to their depots/Business Centers?
- Is the timeline believable?

If all of that is “in process,” they’ll treat you as a **potential future item** and might:

- Ask you to come back when product is in your Everett warehouse, or
- Keep you on file and ask for updates, or
- Tentatively explore item setup with a “subject to samples and documentation” condition.

They generally do not sign firm POs when:

- You don’t yet have US‑landed product, and
- You haven’t shown performance in at least some regional or B2B accounts.

So expect interest and questions, not an immediate commitment.

**How to frame your 3‑month timeline so it sounds credible** 

Position it like this:

1. India-side is **locked**: manufacturer, specs, packaging, certifications, pricing.
2. Import plan is **scheduled**: you know approximate sail date, port of entry (Seattle/Tacoma), customs broker.
3. US-side is **ready**: Everett warehouse, FDA registration in process/complete, insurance, labeling.

You’re basically saying: “We’re not asking you to take risk on our execution. We’re just aligning our production/import schedule with your review cycle.”

**What to actually say in your outreach** 

Very roughly:

- “We are currently finalizing the first production run and container for a 5L cow ghee 2‑pack designed specifically for restaurant and foodservice buyers.”
- “Product is scheduled to land in our Everett, WA warehouse in ~90 days.”
- “I’d like to share specs and pricing now so that, if the item is of interest, we can line up a small regional test as soon as inventory is in the US.”

That tells them: you’re not asking for a PO today; you’re asking for a **review now** to shorten time from landed stock → trial.

**What you should have ready before talking to them** 

Even if inventory is 3 months away, have:

- Draft sell sheet with final specs (5L × 2 pack, case/pallet configuration, ingredients, shelf life, certifications).
- Preliminary landed-cost math to show their margin.
- Clear timeline:

- Production date in India
- Estimated vessel ETD/ETA
- ETA to Everett warehouse

- Status of compliance: FDA facility registration submitted/approved, label review done, lab tests ordered or completed.

If you can say “production starts on [month], container sails around [month], lands at Seattle [month], available for Costco Business Center tests from [month],” it feels concrete.

**The pragmatic play for you** 

Use the next 3 months to:

- Lock in the supply chain and actually get the first container moving.
- Build some early demand data from local stores and restaurants (Everett/Seattle) once it lands.
- In parallel, start the Costco Business Center conversation now as a **Q2/Q3 test opportunity**, not a “we have inventory today” ask.

So: Costco will see a 3‑month lead as normal if you treat it like a scheduled launch window and back it up with specifics, not a vague “in process.”
